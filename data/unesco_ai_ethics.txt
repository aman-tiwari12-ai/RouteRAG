UNESCO RECOMMENDATION ON THE ETHICS OF ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE
Source: UNESCO, November 2021

OVERVIEW
The UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence was adopted on November 24, 2021, by UNESCO's 193 Member States. It is the first global standard-setting instrument on AI ethics adopted by any intergovernmental body. Unlike binding treaties, it is a "recommendation" — expressing consensus without legal obligation.

CORE VALUES

The Recommendation is grounded in four core values:

1. Human Rights and Human Dignity
Every AI actor should respect, protect, and promote human rights and fundamental freedoms. AI should never be used to undermine human dignity.

2. Living in Peaceful, Just, and Interconnected Societies
AI should foster peace and security. States should ensure AI is not weaponised against civilian populations.

3. Ensuring Diversity and Inclusiveness
AI should celebrate human diversity. Systems should avoid perpetuating stereotypes or discrimination.

4. Environment and Ecosystem Flourishing
AI systems should be designed to minimise their environmental impact. Data centres and training compute have significant carbon footprints that must be addressed.

KEY PRINCIPLES

Proportionality and Do No Harm:
AI actors must assess impacts throughout the AI lifecycle. Where significant uncertainty exists about harm, a precautionary approach should be adopted.

Safety and Security:
Unwanted harms from AI should be avoided. Unlike the OECD principles, UNESCO explicitly includes environmental harms in safety considerations.

Fairness and Non-discrimination:
AI systems must not discriminate on grounds including race, gender, language, religion, national or social origin, disability, or other status. Note: UNESCO's list of protected grounds is broader than the EU AI Act's specification.

Sustainability:
Long-term environmental impact of AI must be considered. This is an area where UNESCO diverges from the EU AI Act, which focuses on human-centred harms with limited treatment of environmental impact.

Right to Privacy and Data Protection:
Individuals must have meaningful control over their personal data. AI systems should use privacy-by-design principles.

Human Oversight and Determination:
Humans must retain the ability to override, correct, and shut down AI systems. This aligns with the EU AI Act's human oversight requirements for high-risk systems.

Transparency and Explainability:
AI systems should be transparent about their nature and limitations. Explainability requirements should be proportionate to the stakes involved. A medical diagnosis AI requires more explainability than a music recommendation system.

Responsibility and Accountability:
Humans are always responsible for AI-based decisions. The Recommendation explicitly rejects the notion of AI "personhood" or AI as a legal entity.

AREAS OF APPLICATION

Education:
AI in education should enhance learning without replacing human teachers. Students should be educated about AI. Algorithmic biases in educational AI must be actively countered.

Science and Research:
Open and collaborative approaches to AI research are encouraged. Responsible data sharing should be promoted.

Labour:
Workers must be protected from discriminatory AI in employment. Social protection systems should be adapted for the AI era. Reskilling programs are essential.

Healthcare:
AI health systems should augment human care, not replace it. Patient data privacy must be protected. AI diagnostics require human review before clinical decisions.

GOVERNANCE RECOMMENDATIONS

States should:
- Develop national AI ethics frameworks
- Establish oversight bodies with genuine enforcement power
- Ensure meaningful participation of civil society in AI governance
- Promote AI ethics education
- Conduct regular assessment of AI systems in use

Multi-stakeholder Approach:
Unlike the EU AI Act's primarily top-down regulatory approach, UNESCO recommends governance that explicitly includes civil society, academia, private sector, and affected communities.

ENFORCEMENT
UNESCO has no enforcement mechanism. The Recommendation relies on:
- National implementation
- Peer review mechanisms
- UNESCO monitoring and reporting
- Civil society advocacy

This represents a significant gap compared to the EU AI Act's binding fines. Some scholars argue voluntary frameworks are insufficient for addressing AI harms.

CONTRADICTIONS WITH OTHER FRAMEWORKS
1. UNESCO's broader protected characteristics list vs. EU AI Act's specific list
2. UNESCO's environmental focus vs. EU AI Act's human-centred framing  
3. UNESCO's multi-stakeholder governance vs. EU AI Act's institutional approach
4. UNESCO explicitly rejects AI legal personhood; this aligns with EU but is more explicit
